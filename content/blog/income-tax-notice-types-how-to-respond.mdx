Receiving an income tax notice is one of the most anxiety-inducing experiences for Indian taxpayers. The envelope — or nowadays, the email from `donotreply@incometax.gov.in` — lands and the heart sinks.

Here is the truth: **most income tax notices are not adversarial**. The majority are automated system-generated communications asking for specific information or flagging a discrepancy that can be resolved in minutes online.

This guide explains the 8 most common notice types, what triggers each one, and the exact steps to respond.

---

## Before Anything Else: Verify Authenticity

Every genuine income tax notice can be verified on the Income Tax Portal.

1. Go to [incometaxindiaefiling.gov.in](https://www.incometaxindiaefiling.gov.in/)
2. Navigate to **e-Verify Notice / Order**
3. Enter your PAN, the Document Identification Number (DIN) from the notice, and the issue date

If the notice does not appear on the portal — **it is fake**. Do not respond to it and report it to the cybercrime helpline.

## The 8 Most Common Notice Types

### 1. Intimation under Section 143(1)

**What it is**: An automated processing summary sent after your ITR is processed. It compares what you filed with what the CPC computed.

**Triggers**:
- Arithmetic error in your return
- Tax already deducted (TDS) not matching Form 26AS / AIS
- Mismatch between declared income and third-party data

**What to do**:
- If the demand is correct (you made an error) — pay the demand through the e-Pay Tax facility
- If the computation is wrong — file a **rectification request under Section 154** online within 30 days

> This is the most common notice and usually the least serious. Do not ignore it — even a small demand accrues interest at 1% per month under Section 220(2).

---

### 2. Notice under Section 139(9) — Defective Return

**What it is**: Your ITR was filed but has a technical defect that makes it invalid.

**Triggers**:
- ITR form mismatch (e.g., filed ITR-1 but had capital gains requiring ITR-2)
- Tax payable but self-assessment tax not paid before filing
- Mandatory schedules left blank

**What to do**: Respond **within 15 days** of receiving the notice (or by the date specified). Log in to the e-filing portal, navigate to **e-Proceedings**, and submit the corrected return. If you miss the window, your original return is treated as if never filed.

---

### 3. Notice under Section 142(1) — Inquiry Before Assessment

**What it is**: The Assessing Officer (AO) wants you to produce specific documents or accounts before completing your assessment.

**Triggers**:
- Large transactions in your AIS (Annual Information Statement) not fully reflected in ITR
- Filed return but AO needs supporting documents
- Not filed return despite apparent income

**What to do**: Respond **within the specified time** (usually 15–30 days). Submit the documents through the e-Proceedings portal. Never ignore this — non-compliance leads to best judgment assessment under Section 144.

---

### 4. Notice under Section 143(2) — Scrutiny Assessment

**What it is**: Your case has been selected for detailed scrutiny. The AO will examine your return in depth.

**Triggers**:
- High-value transactions flagged by CASS (Computer-Aided Scrutiny Selection)
- Significant jump in turnover or expenses year-on-year
- Large cash deposits, foreign transactions, or real estate deals

**What to do**: **Engage a CA immediately.** This is not a DIY situation. The scrutiny process involves submitting detailed responses, producing books of accounts, and sometimes appearing before the AO. Timely, accurate responses are critical.

---

### 5. Notice under Section 148 — Escaped Income

**What it is**: The AO believes income has escaped assessment — i.e., you earned income that was not taxed.

**Triggers**:
- Information received from financial institutions, registrars, or other agencies
- Anonymous tip-offs about undisclosed assets
- Cash deposits inconsistent with declared income

**What to do**: This is a serious notice. You have the right to challenge reopening under the newer faceless scheme rules. **Get professional help immediately.** Post the SC ruling in the Union of India vs. Ashish Agarwal case, taxpayers have more protection — old notices issued without proper procedure can be quashed.

---

### 6. Notice under Section 156 — Demand Notice

**What it is**: A formal demand for tax, interest, or penalty after an assessment is completed.

**What to do**: 
- If you agree — pay within **30 days**
- If you disagree — file an appeal before the Commissioner (Appeals) within 30 days and simultaneously apply for stay of demand

---

### 7. Notice under Section 271(1)(c) — Penalty for Concealment

**What it is**: The AO intends to levy penalty for concealment of income or furnishing inaccurate particulars.

**What to do**: The penalty is 100%–300% of tax sought to be evaded. Respond with a detailed written explanation. Penalty can often be avoided if you demonstrate the error was bona fide.

---

### 8. Notice under Section 245 — Refund Adjusted Against Demand

**What it is**: Not technically a notice — the department has adjusted your pending refund against an existing tax demand.

**What to do**: If the demand is valid, accept the adjustment. If not, file a **rectification petition** and submit proof of payment or dispute the underlying demand.

---

## The Golden Rules When You Receive Any Notice

1. **Do not panic** — read it carefully and identify the section under which it is issued
2. **Check the deadline** — every notice has a response date; mark it immediately
3. **Verify on the portal** — confirm the notice is genuine
4. **Do not ignore** — ignoring an income tax notice never makes it go away; it escalates
5. **Preserve all documents** — bank statements, TDS certificates, investment proofs, sale deeds
6. **Get professional help for anything beyond 143(1)** — DIY responses to scrutiny notices often make things worse
