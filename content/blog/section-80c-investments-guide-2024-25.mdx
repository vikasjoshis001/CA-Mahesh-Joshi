Section 80C of the Income Tax Act offers a deduction of up to **₹1,50,000** per financial year — reducing your taxable income by that amount. For someone in the 30% tax bracket, fully using 80C saves ₹46,800 in tax every year (including 4% cess).

Yet a large number of taxpayers either don't fully utilise the limit, or worse, invest in the wrong instruments without considering lock-in, liquidity, or returns.

This guide compares every major 80C instrument so you can choose the right mix.

## ELSS: The Best Option for Most Working Professionals

**Equity Linked Saving Scheme (ELSS)** mutual funds are the only 80C instrument that invests in equities, offering:

- **Shortest lock-in**: 3 years (vs. 5–15 years for everything else)
- **Highest growth potential**: Long-term returns of 12–15% CAGR historically
- **SIP-friendly**: You can invest ₹12,500/month (₹1.5 lakh annual) via SIP and each SIP unit completes its own 3-year lock-in

**Tax on maturity**: Long-term capital gains (LTCG) — but only gains above ₹1 lakh per year are taxed at 10%. Most investors don't hit this threshold in a given year if redemptions are planned well.

**Who should avoid it**: Those very close to needing the money or with low risk tolerance.

## PPF: The Risk-Free Compounder

Public Provident Fund remains the gold standard for **risk-free, tax-free compounding**.

- **Rate**: 7.1% per annum (revised quarterly by the government; has been stable recently)
- **Lock-in**: 15 years, with partial withdrawals allowed from Year 7
- **Tax treatment**: EEE — investment is tax-exempt, interest is tax-exempt, maturity is tax-exempt
- **Limit**: ₹1.5 lakh per year maximum

**Best use case**: Long-term wealth building for goals 15+ years away (children's education, retirement supplement). Open one today if you don't have one — the power of 15-year compounding at 7.1% is significant.

## NPS: The Hidden ₹50,000 Extra Deduction

The National Pension System (NPS) deserves special mention because it offers an **additional ₹50,000 deduction** under Section 80CCD(1B) — over and above the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit.

In total, with NPS you can claim:
- ₹1,50,000 under 80C (shared with other instruments)
- ₹50,000 additional under 80CCD(1B) — NPS only
- **Total: ₹2,00,000 in deductions**

For a 30% taxpayer, that extra ₹50,000 saves an additional **₹15,600** in tax.

**Catch**: NPS has a long lock-in until age 60, and 40% of the corpus must be used to buy an annuity on retirement (annuity income is taxable).

## Life Insurance: Get Insurance Right, Not as a Tax Instrument

Many clients come to me having invested lakhs in traditional endowment or money-back policies purely for 80C benefit. This is a mistake.

Traditional insurance plans give **4–5% returns** — often less than inflation. The insurance cover is also usually inadequate.

**My recommendation**: 
1. Buy a **pure term insurance** plan for adequate life cover (this also qualifies for 80C)
2. Invest the premium difference in ELSS or PPF for far better returns

## How to Build Your 80C Portfolio

Here is a sample allocation strategy for a salaried professional in their 30s:

| Instrument | Amount | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| EPF (mandatory) | ₹60,000–₹80,000 | Already deducted; count it |
| ELSS SIP | ₹40,000–₹60,000 | Fill remaining 80C limit |
| PPF | ₹10,000–₹20,000 | Long-term safety bucket |
| Term insurance premium | ₹10,000–₹15,000 | Life cover + 80C benefit |
| NPS 80CCD(1B) | ₹50,000 | Extra ₹50k deduction |

Most salaried employees find their EPF contributions already fill a significant portion of the ₹1.5 lakh limit. Calculate your EPF contribution first, then fill the remaining limit with ELSS.

## Common Mistakes I See Every Year

1. **Investing in tax-saving FDs at year-end in March** — this forces you to make a one-time lump sum investment rather than a disciplined SIP, and FDs offer the lowest returns
2. **Not accounting for EPF** — your EPF contributions already qualify; don't double-count when planning
3. **Over-insuring with ULIPs** — high charges eat returns; ELSS + term insurance is almost always better
4. **Not using Section 80CCD(1B)** — the additional ₹50,000 NPS deduction is completely separate from 80C and is often overlooked
