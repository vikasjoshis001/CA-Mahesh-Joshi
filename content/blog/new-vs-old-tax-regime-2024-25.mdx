Budget 2024 quietly made the **new tax regime the default** for all taxpayers. If you did not actively choose the old regime when filing your ITR, the system switched you automatically. That single change affects millions of salaried employees and professionals across India.

The question every client asks me is the same: *"Which regime should I be in?"* The honest answer is — it depends on your deductions. Here is a structured breakdown so you can decide.

---

## Tax Slabs at a Glance

### New Tax Regime (FY 2024-25)

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹3,00,000 | Nil |
| ₹3,00,001 – ₹7,00,000 | 5% |
| ₹7,00,001 – ₹10,00,000 | 10% |
| ₹10,00,001 – ₹12,00,000 | 15% |
| ₹12,00,001 – ₹15,00,000 | 20% |
| Above ₹15,00,000 | 30% |

**Rebate under Section 87A**: Nil tax up to ₹7,00,000 income (rebate of up to ₹25,000).  
**Standard Deduction**: ₹75,000 for salaried employees (increased in Budget 2024 from ₹50,000).

### Old Tax Regime (FY 2024-25)

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹2,50,000 | Nil |
| ₹2,50,001 – ₹5,00,000 | 5% |
| ₹5,00,001 – ₹10,00,000 | 20% |
| Above ₹10,00,000 | 30% |

**Standard Deduction**: ₹50,000 for salaried employees.  
**All deductions available**: 80C, 80D, HRA, LTA, home loan interest, etc.

---

## What You Give Up in the New Regime

The new regime offers lower slab rates but **removes most deductions**:

- **Section 80C** (₹1.5 lakh for PF, ELSS, LIC, PPF, home loan principal) — **not available**
- **Section 80D** (health insurance premium up to ₹25,000–₹75,000) — **not available**
- **HRA exemption** — **not available**
- **Home loan interest deduction (Section 24b)** — **not available** for self-occupied property
- **LTA, professional tax, food coupons** — **not available**

---

## The Break-Even Analysis

The new regime wins when your total deductions are **low**. The old regime wins when your deductions are **high**.

Here is a practical comparison for a salaried employee with ₹12 lakh gross income:

**Scenario A — Minimal deductions (₹50,000 standard only)**

| | Old Regime | New Regime |
|---|---|---|
| Gross Income | ₹12,00,000 | ₹12,00,000 |
| Standard Deduction | –₹50,000 | –₹75,000 |
| Taxable Income | ₹11,50,000 | ₹11,25,000 |
| Tax + Cess (approx.) | ₹1,61,200 | ₹93,600 |
| **Savings with New** | | **₹67,600** |

**Scenario B — Maximum deductions (₹2.5 lakh claimed)**

| | Old Regime | New Regime |
|---|---|---|

> **Rule of thumb**: If your total deductions (beyond standard deduction) exceed ₹2,00,000–₹2,50,000, the old regime usually wins for income between ₹10–₹15 lakh.

## Who Should Choose the Old Regime

Consider sticking with the old regime if you:

1. **Have a home loan** — Section 24(b) allows up to ₹2 lakh interest deduction annually on a self-occupied property.
2. **Pay significant health insurance premiums** — A family floater for parents (senior citizens) can alone give you ₹50,000–₹75,000 under 80D.
3. **Invest in 80C instruments** — If you are already contributing to EPF, PPF, or paying LIC premiums, you are close to the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit.
4. **Receive substantial HRA** — Metro city employees with high rent can claim large HRA exemptions.

---

## Who Should Choose the New Regime

The new regime is clearly better if you:

1. **Are a young professional or fresher** with no investments yet.
2. **Earn up to ₹7.75 lakh** — After the ₹75,000 standard deduction, taxable income is ₹7 lakh, attracting zero tax under 87A rebate.
3. **Have a business income** and prefer the simpler compliance (no need to track deductions).
4. **Do not have a home loan** and rent in a tier-2 city where HRA is not substantial.

---

## Important: Business Owners Have a Catch

If you have business or professional income and choose the new regime, you **cannot switch back freely every year**. You get only one chance to switch back to the old regime in your lifetime. Salaried employees are more flexible — they can switch every year.
